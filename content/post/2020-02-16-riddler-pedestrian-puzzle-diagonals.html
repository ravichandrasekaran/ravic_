Re-making urban sidewalks, from the Riddler

Re-making urban sidewalks, from the Riddler

Let’s consider City Hall to be at \((0, 0)\) on the street coordinates. Let’s also consider $p = (p_1, p_2) $ to be the coordinates for nearest street intersection for a home \(h\).

The Manhattan distance \(d_1\) from the nearest street intersection \(p\) to the origin is simply:

\[ \begin{aligned} d_1(p, 0) & = | p_1 | + | p_2 | \\ \end{aligned} \]

Now, let’s consider the new sidewalk layout. The sidewalks are laid out on a grid too, so we can use Manhattan distance here, too. But first, we have to project our points onto new axes, with a scale and rotation.

\[ \begin{aligned} p^{\prime} & = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \\ \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} p_1 \\ p_2 \\ \end{bmatrix} \\ & = \begin{bmatrix} p_1 + p_2 \\ -p_1 + p_2 \\ \end{bmatrix} \\ \end{aligned} \]

And the Manhattan distance of the point projected into the new coordinate system, scaled by the unit length, gives us:

\[ \begin{aligned} d_1^{\prime}(p^{\prime}, 0) & = | p_1^{\prime} | + | p_2^{\prime} | \\ & = | p_1 + p_2 | + | -p_1 + p_2 | \\ \end{aligned} \]

Before we can compare the two distances, we have to account for the different unit lengths in the coordinate systems.

\[ \begin{aligned} u^{\prime} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \\ \end{aligned} \]

So now, we’re comparing the two distances, using the unit length from our first coordinate system, and looking to find where the sidewalk system has a shorter length.

\[ \begin{aligned} d_1(p, 0) & > d^{\prime}(p^\prime, 0) \cdot u^{\prime} \\ | p_1 | + | p_2 | & > [| p_1 + p_2 | + | -p_1 + p_2 |] \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \\ \end{aligned} \]

For a moment, let’s limit ourselves to the first quadrant, so that \(p_1, p_2 \ge 0\). Then:

\[ \begin{aligned} | p_1 | + | p_2 | & > [| p_1 + p_2 | + | -p_1 + p_2 |] \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \\ p_1 + p_2 & > [p_1 + p_2 + | -p_1 + p_2 |] \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \\ (p_1 + p_2)(1 - \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}) & > | -p_1 + p_2 | \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \\ (p_1 + p_2)(1 - \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}) & > (-p_1 + p_2) \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \\ p_1 & > p_2 \cdot (-2 \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} - 1) \\ p_1 & > p_2 \cdot (\sqrt{2} - 1) \\ (p_1 + p_2)(1 - \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}) & > | -p_1 + p_2 | \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \\ (p_1 + p_2)(1 - \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}) & < (p_1 - p_2) \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \\ p_1 & < p_2 \cdot (\sqrt{2} - 1) \\ \end{aligned} \]

The two lines in the first quadrant gives us two angles for the circular city, using \(\arctan\).

\[ \begin{aligned} p_1 & = p_2 \cdot (\sqrt{2} - 1) \\ \frac{p_2}{p_1} & = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} - 1} \\ \tan{\theta_2} & = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} - 1} \\ \theta_2 & = \arctan{(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2} - 1})} \\ \end{aligned} \\ \begin{aligned} p_2 & = p_1 \cdot (\sqrt{2} - 1) \\ \frac{p_2}{p_1} & = \sqrt{2} - 1 \\ \tan{\theta_1} & = \sqrt{2} - 1 \\ \theta_2 & = \arctan{(\sqrt{2} - 1)} \\ \end{aligned} \\ \]
